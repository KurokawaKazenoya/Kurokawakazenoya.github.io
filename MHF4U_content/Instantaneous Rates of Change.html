Instantaneous Rates of Change

What is the instantaneous rate of change?

Suppose you are driving to school.  

  • What would you say your average speed was coming to school (in km/h)?
  • At every point in your journey, was your speed exactly the same as the average?

Instantaneous rate of change is the exact rate of change of a function f(x) at a specific value of the independent variable x= a.

Instantaneous rate of change in Functions

Consider the linear function.  At every point on the graph the slope is the same, so the average rate of change is the same over every interval.  This also means that the instantaneous rate of change is the same at every point on the graph.

On the other hand, the quadratic function has different average rates of change over different intervals.  The instantaneous rate of change at different points will also be different.  For example, at x=-2 you would expect the instantaneous rate of change to be negative and at x=2 you would expect.

How do we estimate the instantaneous rate of change? 

Suppose we want to estimate the instantaneous rate of change of a function f(x) at x= a as shown.  

We know that the average rate of change between P and Q is given by the slope of the secant.  

To find the best estimate for the instantaneous rate of change at x=a, it makes sense to choose P and Q as close together as possible (so, make h as small as possible).

That way the average rate of change can be used to give an estimate of the instantaneous rate of change.

Tangent Lines

A graphical method for finding average rate of change is using secant lines.  

To find an estimate for instantaneous rate of change, we use average rate of change with very small intervals.

Graphically, this just means that we need a secant line where the two points are as close as possible.  In other words, we need tangent lines.

A tangent line is a line that touches the graph at only one point, P, within a small interval of a relation.  The tangent line goes in the same direction as the relation at point P (called the point of tangency).

The tangent line could, but does not have to, cross the graph at another point outside this interval.

Instantaneous Rates of Change Using Graphs

The slope of a tangent at a point on a graph is equivalent to the instantaneous rate of change of a function at this point.

Unfortunately, the slope of a tangent cannot be calculated directly using the slope formula, because the coordinates of only one point are known. 

We can estimate the slope by calculating the slopes of a series of secant lines that go through the fixed point of tangency P and points that get closer and closer to this fixed point, Q1, Q2, and Q3.
