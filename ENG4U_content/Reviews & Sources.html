Reviews & Sources

Conducting the Research

Research helps in developing existing concepts.  This is primarily why we do literature reviews.

     

What Are Literature Reviews?

As Dena Taylor of the Health Sciences Writing Centre of the University of Toronto has written:

“A literature review is an account of what has been published on a topic by accredited scholars and researchers.”

Usually, this takes the form of an annotated bibliography or an introduction to your essay.

Accordion

The Value of Literature Reviews

Literature reviews are written to:

  • Acknowledge the works of other authors regarding your topic.
  • Identify the strengths and weaknesses of existing concepts related to your topic.
  • Set the groundwork and context for your study.

To Develop a Literature Review, Start With The Questions

  • What is a specific thesis, problem, or research question that my literature review helps to define?
  • What type of literature review am I conducting? 
    • Am I looking at issues of theory? methodology? policy? quantitative research (e.g. on the effectiveness of a new procedure)? qualitative research (e.g., studies of loneliness among migrant workers)?
  • What is the scope of my literature review? What types of publications am I using (e.g., journals, books, government documents, popular media)? 
  • What discipline am I working in? (e.g., nursing psychology, sociology, medicine)

More Questions to Ask When Developing Literature Reviews

  • How good was my information seeking? Has my search been wide enough to ensure I’ve found all the relevant material? Has it been narrow enough to exclude irrelevant material? Is the number of sources
  • I’ve used appropriate for the length of my paper?
  • Have I critically analysed the literature I use? Do I follow through a set of concepts and questions, comparing items to each other in the ways they deal with them? Instead of just listing and summarizing items, do I assess them, discussing strengths and weaknesses?
  • Have I cited and discussed studies contrary to my perspective?
  • Will the reader find my literature review relevant, appropriate, and useful?

Literature Review Information Comes From Sources:

There are two types of sources: primary and secondary.

 

Primary Sources  Secondary Sources
  • Information you get firsthand
  • If you do the work → it’s a primary source

Examples:

  • Correspondence between you and an expert on your topic
  • Interviews with subjects
  • Experiments
  • Research that has been conducted by others, and is published by others

Examples:

  • Journal articles
  • Quotations from experts

Credibility of Sources is Very Important

Accordion

Reputable organization

  • Make sure the source comes from a known organization.

Respected author

  • Check the author’s background.

Comprehensive bibliography

  • Check the article’s supported sources.

Grammatically sound articles

  • There are little to no grammar errors in the text.

Updated information

  • Make sure the information is timely and relevant.

Peer-reviewed

  • Make sure other organizations or authors reviewed these articles.
