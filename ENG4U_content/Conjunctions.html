Conjunctions

Conjunctions are a part of speech. They link words together with other words, phrases, or clauses.

  • Conjunctions allow you to create complex sentences by connecting trains of thought.
  • There are three types of conjunctions.

    1. Coordinating conjunctions

    2. Correlative conjunctions

    3. Subordinating conjunctions

Coordinating Conjunctions

  • There are seven coordinating conjunctions.
  • These are used between phrases, clauses, or words. 

Remember the acronym:

F.A.N.B.O.Y.S.

Which stands for: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so.

F.A.N.B.O.Y.S

“For” is used when explaining the reason for something.

  • Spock used the universal translator for decoding unknown alien language.

“And” is used when adding a phrase, clause, or word to another phrase, clause, or word.

  • Captain Kirk was pretty handy with a phaser, and a great pilot too!

“Nor” is used when adding a phrase or clause with a negative idea to another phrase or clause with a negative idea.

  • Bones does not want to talk to Spock, nor does he want to see him any time soon.

“But” is used to show differences between two things.

  • The USS Enterprise is not the largest ship, but it is certainly the fastest.

“Or” is used to show an alternative.

  • Captain Kirk knew he had to make the decision soon, send a friendly message , or shoot at a possible hostile enemy.  

“Yet” is used to provide a contrast to the first statement.

  • Spock has challenges in showing human emotion, yet it didn’t stop Kirk from befriending him. 

“So” is used to show a result of an event. It is also used to show the consequence of something.

  • Spock is really smart, so Kirk made him Science officer.

Correlative Conjunctions

  • These are pairs of conjunctions that are used together to connect two phrases or clauses.
  • Common correlative conjunctions are:
  • Either/or - this is used in a sentence to show that two things are of the same level and that both of the choices are interchangeable.
  • e.g. She gave him a choice to either stay at home or come with her to the party.

  • Common correlative conjunctions are:

  • Neither/nor - this is used in a sentence to show that two or more negative alternatives. It is used in a negative sense.
  • e.g. Neither Luke, nor Lea knew Darth Vader was their father. 
  • Not only/but also - this is used to connect two phrases, clauses, or words in the same level.
  • e.g. Jim not only draws well, but also does voice acting on the side. 

  • Common correlative conjunctions are:

  • as/as is used to compare two things.
  • “Saruman was as strong as Gandalf.”
  • As + adjective + as
  • both/and is used to show that two things are equal.
  • “Anakin Skywalker was both a Jedi and a disciple of the dark side.”

Subordinating Conjunctions

  • These are conjunctions that join independent clauses and dependent clauses together. This type of conjunction links two unequal elements.
  • Subordinating conjunctions function as a transition between two ideas, or as a clause that reduces the importance of the other.
  • Some examples of subordinating conjunctions are:
  • After, although, as, because, before, even if, even though, if, in order to, once, provided that, rather than, since, so that, than, that, though, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whereas, whenever, whether, while, why.

  • There are four ways you can construct a sentence using a subordinating conjunction.
  • You can use a subordinating conjunction in linking:
  • An independent clause and a dependent clause
  • A dependent clause and an independent clause
  • A main clause and an essential relative clause
  • A main clause and a non-essential relative clause

Independent and Dependent Clauses

Independent and dependent clauses are the two main categories of clause. A clause is a group of words that contains at least one subject and at least one verb. Clauses can either be complete or incomplete sentences, depending on their wording and punctuation.

  • If the clause alone is a complete sentence, it is an independent clause.
  • If the clause alone is not a complete sentence, it is a dependent clause.
  • Dependent clauses are also known as Subordinate clauses and they are usually easy to recognize, as they have a subordinate conjunction like because, if or whenever.

These are used to join a dependent and an independent clause.

Subordinating Conjunctions

  • An independent clause and a dependent clause - this is a simple sentence, no comma is required.
  • Kirk still sent a friendly message even though the other ship showed signs of hostility.
  • A dependent clause and an independent clause - the comma comes after the end of the dependent clause.
  • After the USS Enterprise landed, Spock immediately went in search of a life form. 

  • A main clause and an essential relative clause - this uses a relative pronoun such as where, who, that, or which. These conjunctions do not require a comma around it.
  • Kirk brought the life form  that their ship detected.
  • A main clause and a non-essential relative clause - this also makes use of a relative pronoun. Although when a relative pronoun follows a noun, it is non-essential, and requires commas if it interrupts the main sentence.
  • Spock, who was the Science officer at that time, advised against the ship going into warp drive.
