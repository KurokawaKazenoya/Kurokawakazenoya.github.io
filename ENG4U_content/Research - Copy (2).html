What is a literature review?

As Dena Taylor of the Health Sciences Writing Centre of the University of Toronto has written:

 “A literature review is an account of what has been published on a topic by accredited scholars and researchers.”

Usually, this takes the form of an annotated bibliography or an introduction to your essay.

Why do you need to write a literature review?

You write a literature review to:

  • Acknowledge the works of other authors regarding your topic.
  • Identify the strengths and weaknesses of existing concepts related to your topic.
  • Set the groundwork and context for your study.

To develop a literature review, you can ask yourself the following questions:

What is a specific thesis, problem, or research question that my literature review helps to define?
What type of literature review am I conducting? Am I looking at issues of theory? methodology? policy? quantitative research (e.g. on the effectiveness of a new procedure)? qualitative research (e.g., studies of loneliness among migrant workers)?
What is the scope of my literature review? What types of publications am I using (e.g., journals, books, government documents, popular media)? 
What discipline am I working in (e.g., nursing psychology, sociology, medicine)?

How good was my information seeking? Has my search been wide enough to ensure I’ve found all the relevant material? Has it been narrow enough to exclude irrelevant material? Is the number of sources I’ve used appropriate for the length of my paper?
Have I critically analysed the literature I use? Do I follow through a set of concepts and questions, comparing items to each other in the ways they deal with them? Instead of just listing and summarizing items, do I assess them, discussing strengths and weaknesses?
Have I cited and discussed studies contrary to my perspective?
Will the reader find my literature review relevant, appropriate, and useful?

PRIMARY SOURCES

  • These are the information you get first hand.
  • The key here is you. If you did the work, then it’s a primary source.
  • Examples:
    • Correspondence between you and an expert on your topic
    • Interviews with subjects
    • Experiments

SECONDARY SOURCES

  • These are research conducted and published by others.
  • Examples:
    • Journal Articles
    • Quotes from Experts

Sources may take different forms: 

  • Print
  • Media
  • Online

Print sources

  • Any source that is public.
    • Examples: Reference books, Books, Periodicals, Journals, Newspapers. 

Media sources

  • These are information taken from any media.
    • Examples: Audio recordings, video recordings, CDs, DVD Videos.

Online sources

  • Information from the internet.
    • Examples: Ebooks, Journal articles from the academic journal platforms, Podcasts, Youtube videos, Tweets. 

In choosing sources, you don’t just get whatever source related to your topic.

There are things you need to consider.

Finding credible sources

Reputable organization →  Make sure the source comes from a known organization.
Respected author → Check the author’s background.
Comprehensive bibliography → Check the article’s supported sources.
Grammatically sound articles → There are little to no grammar errors in the text.
Updated information →  Make sure the information is timely and relevant.
Peer-reviewed → Make sure other organizations or authors reviewed these articles.

Qualitative and Quantitative Research Methods

Researchers use different methods to collect quantitative and qualitative data. The data collected will answer the research question. 

Today, we will explore the most common methods that you can use for your research. 

Quantitative Research Methods

Surveys, experiments, and observations are the most common methods to collect data for qualitative research. 

Before we take a look at how each quantitative research method is used, list down all the words that pop into your head when you hear the word survey, experiment, and observation. 

What is a survey? Application of survey research Application of experimental research

A survey is a quantitative research method where a set of multiple-choice questions is distributed to an entire group or sample of individuals. Surveys can be done online, in-person, or over the phone.

Surveys are often used to explain trends or features of large groups.
It may also be used to plan a more in-depth study of a research topic. 

Countries routinely conduct surveys worldwide to collect and analyze data to make informed policy decisions, allocate resources, and assess national needs.

Experiments are widely used in social sciences, psychology, and education. 

If you could conduct an experiment to dispute a currently known fact, what would it be and why?

What are observations?

This is the research method used to identify a behavior or occurrence of interest and monitor it in its natural setting.

For example, to study classroom participation, a researcher sits in one of the classes to observe students. The researcher will observe, count, and record the behaviors of students from different backgrounds. 

Qualitative research methods

The most common qualitative research methods are interviews, focus groups, ethnography, and literature reviews. 

We will find out how each one is done. 

What is an interview?

In an interview, the researcher asks open-ended questions verbally to respondents. Interviews can be structured, unstructured, and semi-structured. 

What are focus groups?

Involves several individuals discussing a topic. It is used to gather opinions that can be used for further research. 

Before a company releases a new product, film, or TV show, they conduct focus groups to determine how the population would potentially react.

Analyzing Quantitative and Qualitative Data

Now that you know the different quantitative and qualitative methods you could use in your research, the next step is to interpret the data you have collected. The method of analysis differs for each type of research method. 

How do I analyze quantitative data?

Quantitative data is based on numbers. Advanced statistical analysis is used to discover patterns in the data. The results are often reported in graphs and tables. 

How do I analyze qualitative data?

Analyzing qualitative data is more challenging because the responses are not standard. It involves identifying patterns, tracking occurrences of words and phrases in the responses, and studying their context. 

Analyzing Qualitative data

There are various approaches to qualitative data analysis, but they all share five steps in common:

  1. Prepare and organize your data.
  2. Review and explore your data.
  3. Develop a data coding system.
  4. Assign codes to the data.
  5. Identify recurring themes.

The specifics of each step depend on the focus of the analysis.
