Counter Arguments

What do you think a “Counter Argument” is?

 

What is a Counter Argument? What is the Purpose of Counter Arguments? Addressing counter arguments shows your audience that:
  • A counterargument is an argument or set of reasons put forward to oppose an idea or theory developed in another argument.
  • In other words, a counterargument is your rebuttal to a person who disagrees with your position.

Knowing how to organize and plan a counter argument has many practical applications

-both in academia and in your day-to-day life

-you recognize that other positions exist
-you have considered these positions
-you can respond to them.

This strengthens your own position (argument) and gives you credibility

Taking Notes for Counter Arguments

When listening to develop a counterargument, taking notes helps you format an effective response to your opponent's key points
  • One way of taking organized and useful notes to develop counterarguments is to divide your paper into two columns.
  • In the first column, jot down the key arguments and points you want to respond to while avoiding writing down everything your opponent says.
  • Use the second column to write down the points you want to make in response. Use arrows to connect ideas and show relationships.

Counter Arguments Made About Terin Izil’s TalkF

  • Dorothy: This argument is limited because everyone doesn’t know the reference to “The Wizard of Oz.” It is also exaggerated as this film appealed to an audience of theatergoers during the Depression. Many in the audience were children, and the education level of the audience was lower than it is today, so Izil ignored any discussion of the audience or context.
  • "In most cases, ten dollar words fail": Izil protects herself against generalizing by saying "in most cases," but aren't there times when "ten dollar words" are absolutely essential in science, for instance? Would it be helpful if surgeons referred to an organ in the body as "a red or blue thing"?
  • Simple words are effective in moving people to act as Patrick Henry is doing, but isn't communication meant to do other things in addition to evoking strong emotions?
  • Izil says that "bigger isn't always better" when it comes to words, but a limited vocabulary limits our understanding and variety as learners and communicators. Isn't it important to have words of all types if we are going to be complex in our thinking and speaking?

Counter Arguments Made About Terin Izil’s Talk, cont.

  • Most of us don't have the luxury of a captive audience, true, but we do have audiences, and if we hope to interest them, we have to have more than one syllable of words, or we'll sound less educated and varied in our thinking than we really are.
  • If we are competing in a whole world of distractions, we'll have to be uniquely interested in our communication, and that interest won't develop when only one-syllable words are used. We'll be repetitive and predictable, even boring, to our audience.
  • The winners at Scrabble have the most innovative use of language at their disposal.
  • "Variety is the spice of life," as Izil says; "bread and butter" are simply "blah," "blah," being another one-syllable word that Izil might find useful!
