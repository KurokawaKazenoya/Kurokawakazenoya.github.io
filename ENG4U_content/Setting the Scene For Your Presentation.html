Setting the Scene For Your Presentation 

 

  • Mis-èn-scene means to “put in the scene”: that is, to create a scene which allows you to present your ideas with clarity and emphasis.
  • By eliminating objects and events that distract your audience, you stand a much better chance of informing others effectively.
  • “Less is more,” everything you include in your presentation has a purpose: reinforcing your ideas. 

   

Why Are You Speaking?

Discourse has two things in common—an audience and a purpose. 

YouTube videos contain others’ ideas and commentaries on virtually every imaginable subject, some of the presentations being memorable, many or most of them being entirely forgettable and self-serving.

Speeches have three main purposes: 

✔️to inform       ✔️to persuade
✔️to entertain

Speeches to Inform

  • Speeches that convey information that range in scope from a brief notice to a lengthy lecture.

Examples

  • Testimonies in court
  • high school presentations
  • YouTube videos on how to do something

Speeches to Persuade

  • These speeches influence the beliefs or actions of audiences. 

Examples

  • Commercials
  • political speeches
  • protest cries
  • sales pitches
  • job interviews

Speeches to Entertain

  • These speeches draw listeners together to share an emotional experience of some sort

Examples

  • Wedding speeches
  • Toasts
  • awards ceremonies
  • convocation speeches

  

Why Are They Listening?
  • When you consider the purpose of a speech and the oral presentation you will produce, think about the needs of the intended audience
  • The key question: "Why are they listening," might be better phrased, "Why should they listen?" 
  • Consider where you are (the setting) and what your audience hopes to learn from your work.
  • When you know your audience, understand why they are listening, and are aware of techniques you might use to heighten their interest, you can create a more effective presentation.

     

Using Inclusive Language

  • Your presentation is a conversation.
  • Nothing makes listeners feel more left out than when they feel as if you are not making them part of the conversation
  • When you limit your references to the audience by addressing your listeners on the basis of gender, race, ethnicity, or another characteristic, you automatically reduce your appeal, and you cannot risk that if you hope to be successful
  • Use inclusive language; that is, include everyone in the conversation by using general terms that make your listeners feel as if they are welcome participants in the great work you are sharing with them.

Third Step in Summative Task

Film Your Presentation

  • Your main task is to create an oral presentation based on your topic and speech.
  • Video record yourself, using a device such as a laptop, tablet or phone.
  • You may need to practice/rehearse a couple of times to feel comfortable and get in a good groove
  • Your presentation should be 2-4 minutes in length.
  • Feel free to use visual aids in your presentation, should it add to your speech (but do not include anything that is overly distracting)

Final Step in Summative Task

Review and Finalize Your Presentation

  • After you have filmed your presentation, make sure to review it until you are satisfied with the finished product.
  • You may add any editing features to your video.
  • Ensure that your video is in mp4 format, and submit to your folder.
