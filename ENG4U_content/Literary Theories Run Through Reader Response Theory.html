Literary Theories Run Through: Reader Response Theory

Reader Response Theory

This theory focuses on how the reader perceives it instead of what the author intends. The text itself has no meaning until a reader reads it.

To apply this theory, we will rely on a close examination of character and motivations and their symbolic meanings (impulses, desires, feelings) that might be linked with the author. 

In this theory, the reader takes an active role in interpreting the text.

 

As a critic, you have to examine the scope and variety of reader reactions, then analyze how different readers, sometimes called “interpretive communities,” make meaning out of the text. Purely personal reactions and inherited or culturally conditioned ways of reading may affect the interpretation of the text.

Lord of the Flies as a dystopian novel

 

In Lord of the Flies, William Golding conducts a literary social experiment showing how humans conditioned by the “primeval evil” spoil their chance of a new beginning and instead create a savage dystopian society. 

 

The relationship dynamic between the characters is often compared by readers to politics, with the conch often said to symbolize democracy, the rule of law, and due process.
