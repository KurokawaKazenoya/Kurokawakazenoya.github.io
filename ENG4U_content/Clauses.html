CLAUSES

These are combinations of words and phrases that give either a complete or incomplete thought. 

  • Usually, you know it is a clause when a subject and a verb appear together. 

Example: 

I cannot remember what happened last Sunday. 

Subject: what

Verb: happened

There are two main types of clauses:

Independent Clause and Dependent Clause

Open All Panels

The Independent Clause

  • This type of clause can stand alone because it presents a complete thought or idea.

Examples:

  • Rick is a scientist.
  • Morty wants to join Rick in his adventures.

The Dependent Clause

  • This type of clause shows an incomplete thought or idea and needs an independent clause for it to make sense.

Examples: 

  • Since I wasn’t feeling well, I decided to take some time off work. 
  • Morty left early to catch the bus to school.

Now that you know what the two main types of clauses are, let’s focus on the types of dependent clauses.

Noun Clauses Adjective Clauses Adverb Clauses

Examples:

  • The most valuable gift that you could ever receive is friendship. (subject)
  • She asked him who cleaned her room this morning. (direct object)
  • This dependent clause functions as an adjective. This means that it describes a noun or pronoun.
  • It usually starts with a relative pronoun (who, which, whose, when, where, that)

Examples:

She listened to the song which he recommended to her.
