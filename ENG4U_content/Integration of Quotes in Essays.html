Integration of Quotes in Essays

To answer the question, you must know how to quote a literary work properly and integrate it naturally into your essay.

Quoting a literary work is essential if it helps your point. 
A quotation provides support, although it needs an introduction and a conclusion that clarifies your use.

The quotation acts as your “proof.”

There are three ways you can integrate quotations into your essays.

  • Summarization
  • Paraphrasing
  • Direct quotations

Remember that it is essential to cite the source of your quotation.

It is also essential to add the text you’re citing in “Works Cited” using the MLA style.

This is done by adding an in-text citation after a summary, paraphrase, or direct quotation.

Follow these instructions in adding in-text citations.

  • (Add the name of the author + Page number)
  • (“Title of the selection” + Page number)
  • Add these after you use a quotation from another source. Enclose it in a parenthesis.

For example, if the author’s name is given:

  • “I am writing a summary” (Smith 1)
  • If the author’s name is not given, use the title of the selection with quotation marks.
  • “I am writing a summary” (“Writing Summaries” 1)

Summarization

This involves rephrasing the main idea and the main points using your own words.

In summarizations, page numbers are not usually needed since the work is being discussed as a whole rather than selecting a passage from it.

Source: https://davidson.libguides.com/c.php?g=349327&p=2361763

Example: 

Environmental conditions in North Carolina pose a threat to frogs and toads. According to scientists, wetland destruction has already occurred on a massive scale. The destruction of wetlands, along with forests, uplands, and migration routes, could destroy vast numbers of these species. This is not only a concern in North Carolina, as frog and toad populations are declining around the world (Dorcas 8).

Source: https://davidson.libguides.com/c.php?g=349327&p=2361763

Paraphrasing

  • This is your version of the main idea and essential information. It is more detailed than a summary, which focuses only on the main idea.
  • Paraphrasing also helps you understand the meaning of the original text.
  • In the third season, Giles was officially relieved from his Watcher duties, but he ignores that and continues as Buffy's trainer, confidant, and father-figure.
  • Here is an example of paraphrasing:
  • Despite his termination by the Watcher's Council in season three, Giles persists to teach and counsel Buffy while playing a "father-figure" role (DeCandido 44).

Source: https://web.williams.edu/wp-etc/acad-resources/survival_guide/CitingDoc/ParaphrasingMLA.php

Direct Quotation

  • You do this primarily by directly quoting the author. The lifted text is then placed in between quotation marks (“,”).
  • For example: “The dream of the whale filled him. Avi felt his body to be suffused, like a peach soaked with brandy, the smell and flavor of the dream more present than the corporeal self” (Parker 40).

How to integrate a quotation into an essay

Open All Panels

First, introduce the author of the quotation you will use.

  • Let the reader know who said the quotation before stating the actual quotation. You also want to use other words for “say” to avoid redundancy.
  • For example, if it is the first time you are quoting the author:

Sean O’Faolain, author of the “The Fur Coat,” writes, “A fur coat, he had learned, is not a grand thing – it is just a useful thing.”

  • But, if it is for a quotation later in the essay:

O’Faolain also states, “All the other women in the world can’t all have time to dress. There must be some way out of it. .” 

Second, state the quotation you are using.

  • The quotation should flow logically and grammatically with your essay. It can disrupt the topic if it is too long or too short.
  • How you quote also depends on the length of what you are quoting.

For short quotations, the text lifted from the source is placed between quotation marks. The author and the page number is also added after the quotation, when applicable.

Example:

Gass and Varonis found that “the listener’s familiarity with the topic of discourse greatly facilitates the interpretation of the entire message” (85).

Source: https://guides.libraries.psu.edu/mlacitation/intext#:~:text=Using%20In%2Dtext%20Citation&text=MLA%20in%2Dtext%20citation%20style,parenthetical%20citation%3A%20(Smith).

  • A quotation with more than three sentences is only used if it is truly needed to support your argument.
    • In MLA, block quotations or long quotations are used in quoting poetry and prose.
      • Block quotations are used for more than four lines of prose.
      • Block quotations are used for more than three lines of poetry.

  • For example:
    At the end of Lord of the Flies the boys are struck with the realization of their behaviour:

    The tears began to flow and sobs shook him. He gave himself up to them now for the first time on the island; great, shuddering spasms of grief that seemed to wrench his whole body. His voice rose under the black smoke before the burning wreckage of the island; and infected by that emotion, the other little boys began to shake and sob too. (Golding 186)

Source: https://columbiacollege-ca.libguides.com/mla/in-text

When quoting a part of a long passage, an ellipsis is used.

For example

  • Original sentence: “In addition to awareness-raising, practicing listening to accented speech has been shown to improve listening comprehension. This article recommends developing listening training programs for library faculty and staff, based on research from the linguistics and language teaching fields. Even brief exposure to accented speech can help listeners improve their comprehension, thereby improving the level of service to international patrons.” (O'Malley 19)
  • Into: “In addition to awareness-raising,practicing listening to accented speech has been shown to improve listening comprehension...Even brief exposure to accented speech can help listeners improve their comprehension.”

Source: https://guides.libraries.psu.edu/mlacitation/intext

Third, summarize the quotation.

  • This is only necessary if the quotation is hard to understand or if it needs an explanation.
  • As a guide, remember that you and the reader need to have the same understanding of the quotation’s importance.

Fourth, analyze or paraphrase the quotation.

  • In analyzing a quotation, a breakdown of that information is needed.
  • Here, you need to examine the parts of the quotation in supporting your argument.
  • Find keywords in the quotation and use that to explain your analysis further and provide your own meaning for the quotation.

Lastly, explain the relevance of the quotation to your essay.

  • This is the culminating piece in quoting another source of information. This part ties the ideas of the quotation together with your argument.
  • Remember to explain the significance of the quotation to your argument.

Remember to have an in-text citation of the quotation that you are integrating inside the paragraph.
