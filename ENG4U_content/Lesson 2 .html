Lesson

In this lesson, students delve into the complexities of personal identity, discussing challenges posed by societal expectations, while exploring the influence of cultural norms on identity formation. Simultaneously, they master the nuanced usage of the subjunctive mood, expressing wishes, doubts, and hypothetical scenarios with accuracy. Additionally, students refine their writing skills by constructing well-organized paragraphs, incorporating clear topic sentences, compelling supporting details, and seamless transitions for coherent and articulate expression.

What's the Big Idea?

Students will examine the challenges individuals face in finding their identity and a sense of belonging in diverse societies.

Success Criteria

  • Analyze the concept of belonging and its relationship with societal norms

  • Use the subjunctive mood accurately in expressing wishes, doubts, and hypothetical situations

  • Develop clear and concise topic sentences that convey the main idea of each paragraph.

  • Incorporate effective transitions between sentences and paragraphs to ensure smooth and logical flow of ideas.

Contextualizing Grammar

Subjunctive Mood: Understanding its usage in expressing wishes, doubts, and hypothetical situations.

Subjunctive Mood Top 5 List

Carousel

Select 'Next' or 'Previous' to progress through slides.

  1. Expresses Hypothetical Situations: The subjunctive mood is used to express hypothetical or unreal situations, wishes, doubts, suggestions, or actions that have not yet occurred. It is often employed to talk about events that are contrary to reality or are unlikely to happen.

  1. Used in Contrary-to-Fact Statements: Subjunctive mood is used in contrary-to-fact statements, indicating conditions that are not true or have not happened. For example, "If I were you, I would study harder" employs the subjunctive mood ("were" instead of "was") to express a hypothetical situation.

  1. Influence of Emotion, Necessity, or Request: Subjunctive mood is used in expressions of emotion, necessity, or request. For instance, "It's essential that he be here on time" uses the subjunctive mood ("be" instead of "is") to convey the necessity of his presence.

  1. Triggered by Certain Verbs, Adjectives, and Nouns: Certain verbs, adjectives, and nouns trigger the subjunctive mood. Expressions like "suggest that," "recommend that," "important that," and phrases implying doubt or uncertainty often lead to the use of the subjunctive mood in the subordinate clause of a sentence.
  1. Commonly Found in Formal or Old-Fashioned Language: While the subjunctive mood is used less frequently in everyday conversation, it is still prevalent in formal writing, literature, and older texts. In modern English, the indicative mood (standard verb forms) often replaces the subjunctive in many contexts, making it less noticeable in casual speech.
