Lesson

Students delve into the intricate web of personal and cultural identity, engaging in thoughtful discussions about the profound influences of race, gender, sexuality, and socioeconomic background. Emphasizing the complexities and intersections of these identities, students critically analyze the multifaceted nature of individual and collective selfhood. Simultaneously, they master the nuances of advanced punctuation, exploring the usage of semicolons, colons, dashes, and ellipses in crafting complex sentence structures. Additionally, students delve into the realm of rhetorical devices, uncovering the power of metaphors, similes, and personification to enhance sentence structure. By infusing their writing with figurative language and depth, students not only refine their language skills but also elevate the artistic expression of their ideas, fostering a deeper understanding of identity and language in the process

What's the Big Idea?

Students will analyze and discuss the impact of race, gender, sexuality, and socioeconomic background on personal and cultural identity.

Success Criteria

  • Effectively utilize semicolons, colons, dashes, and ellipses in complex sentence structures, displaying mastery in punctuating sentences for enhanced clarity and emphasis in writing.

  • Analyze and articulate the influences of various identity factors, as well as the impact of advanced punctuation and rhetorical devices 

  • Skillfully incorporate metaphors, similes, and personification in their writing, enhancing sentence structure with figurative language

Contextualizing Grammar

Advanced Punctuation: Exploring ellipses and dashes in complex sentence structures.

Ellipses are used to indicate a pause, an omission of words, or a trailing off in thought. They create a sense of suspense or reflection. Let's look at an example from "Totem Poles and Railroads":

"The history of totem poles is fascinating... it tells stories of Indigenous communities and their connection to nature."

In this sentence, the ellipsis creates a pause before revealing the significance of totem poles.

Dashes are used to indicate a sudden change in thought, provide emphasis, or set off additional information within a sentence. Here's an example from "The Firekeeper's Daughter":

In this sentence, the dash adds emphasis to the importance of the protagonist's family as her support system.
