Note-Taking & Active Listening Skills

   

Links Between Taking Notes and Listening Actively  Active vs. Passive Listening
  • In university, there are people who were clearly brilliant but who had a really difficult time taking good notes.
  • Similarly, many profess that note-taking was not their strong suit. (Some of them tried to write down everything the professor said, missed important points, and became frustrated.
  • They would look back at their notes and have the beginnings of full sentences written down.)
  • Others thought it was more important to just listen and understand the lecture and didn't write anything down.
  • They forgot the important points or large parts of the lecture.
  • It is worthwhile, then, to spend some time developing good note–taking skills before you are off to university.
  • Developing good note–taking skills, however, is linked with learning how to listen.

Listening is an active process, and we employ (consciously or subconsciously) different strategies when we are listening for different purposes.

  • We've all had the experience of realizing we haven't really heard a word a person has said, after all.
  • The way you listen to something recreationally is different from the way you listen to a class lecture.
  • The way you listen when you are in a debate or argument is different from the way you listen when you are expected to develop questions to ask about the presentation.
  • Further, in academic settings, you may be listening to write a summary, gather specific information, formulate an opinion, or develop a counter-argument.
  • How you listen directly informs what you choose to write down.
  • If you don't know how to determine the important points in a lecture, chances are your notes aren't going to be very helpful to you. These two skills are intertwined.

   

In this lesson we are going to focus on developing active listening strategies and good note–taking skills.

Accordion

Active Listening Skills - Why Are You Listening?

  • Good listening really begins with motivation. If you know why you are listening, and why it is important, you become an active rather than a passive listener.
  • Of course, not every subject is inherently interesting for everyone, but even if the motivation is that you need to understand something in order to do well in a course or assignment, that's a reason!
  • Sitting in lecture is certainly more enjoyable if you can find some reason to be interested in the subject, however.

Active Listening - It’s Up To You

  • You are expected to take an interest in and understand the speaker.
  • Learning is up to the learner.
  • If you simply want to sit passively and blame the speaker for your lack of success, then you're not a serious learner.
  • It sounds obvious, but the more effort you put into listening, the more you will learn. It's all up to you.

Active Listening - Minimize Distractions

  • If you can't hear, arrange things so you can. Move away from sources of noise -- human or mechanical.
  • Sit where you can see the speaker easily and where other distractions are at a minimum.
  • It's actually a documented fact that students who sit closer to the front of the room get better marks.
  • There are surely several factors involved, but there are fewer distractions at the front of the class where it's easier to pay attention.

Active Listening - Listen Without Judgment

  • Listen to what the speaker is saying.
  • Don't tune the speaker out because you don't like something about the presenter or the message.
  • Be sure you understand something before you reject it.
  • You can definitely make note of questions or comments as you go, but don't become so distracted by something you disagree with that you miss out on the rest of the information.
  • Don't listen with your emotions but with your intellect.

Active Listening - Look for the Pattern

  • Look for the speaker's pattern of organization.
  • In a lecture, a speaker is generally referring to notes or some other source of information.
  • You can understand much better if you are able to recognize what the speaker's driving at and how the speaker's getting there.
  • Recognizing the pattern also helps you organize your notes.

Active Listening - Listen for the Main Ideas

  • Look for the main idea or ideas in the presentation. (Facts are important only in their support of the speaker's points. How do you know what is important and what is trivial?)
  • Often, speakers stress important information in their lecture/presentation. If you can recognize this, it will help you listen for the main ideas. (Pay attention to the presenter's tendency to rephrase, repeat, or summarize ideas. If the speaker uses these techniques, consider those points worthy of writing down. Most likely, those ideas are central to your understanding of the presentation.)
  • Other non-verbal cues include gesturing with the hands, raising the voice, and pausing. We will be looking at some examples of presentations to discuss these cues in more depth.

Active Listening - The Mind is Faster than the Mouth

  • The average presenter speaks at about 125 words per minute, but the mind can take in words at the rate of 400 to 500 in the same amount of time.
  • This is part of the reason it is so easy for the mind to wander.
  • If you can harness that power, though, and resist stray thoughts, you can make use of the space between words to organize your notes, make comments, and ultimately gain much more from the lectures you attend.

Active Listening - Take Notes

  • Take notes while you listen.
  • Even if you recognize everything being said and you think you'll remember everything that is said, take notes because you won't remember all of the details in the presentations later.
