Oral Fluency

Now that you have learned more words and gained an idea of how to choose them, we will now know how to express these words when speaking fluently.

What is oral fluency?

Fluency is the smoothness or flow with which sounds, syllables, words and phrases are said when speaking. When you can speak and understand a language with ease, you are fluent in that language.

Why is oral fluency important?

Having fluent speech is important so that we can effectively express our thoughts and ideas. It is also an essential tool for socializing.

Building blocks of fluency

To achieve oral fluency, you must practice proper stress, chunking and intonation when you speak. Let’s examine each block and start you on your journey in improving your oral fluency.

Stress

Stress is the emphasis that we place on a specific syllable of a word or word in a sentence.

Word Stress

The stressed syllable is said in a slightly higher pitch to place emphasis. Here are some examples.

PO-li-tics → the stress is in the first syllable

tech-NIQUE → the stress is in the last syllable

pro-DUC-tion → stress is the second syllable

Sentence Stress

Sentence stress means you emphasize a specific word and not just a syllable. Doing so can drastically change the meaning of a sentence. Let’s take a look at the simple sentence, “he was angry.”

He was angry  → emphasizes who was angry. 

He was angry  → emphasizes when he was angry. 

He was angry  → emphasizes what the actor felt.

Chunking

Chunking is breaking specific words into groups and pausing briefly after each group. Doing this can help deliver a more straightforward message.

Intonation

Intonation is just like stress. It is the way our voice rises and falls when speaking.

Just as words have stressed syllables, sentences have regular patterns depending on the meaning or feeling we want to convey.

Intonation patterns

There are two basic patterns of intonation in English: rising (↗) and falling intonation (↘).

 

Rising Intonation

Rising intonation (↗) is when the voice rises at the end of the sentence. It invites the speaker to continue talking. 

 

The rising intonation is commonly used in asking yes or no questions. 

Intonation is important because it conveys meaning in many ways.
