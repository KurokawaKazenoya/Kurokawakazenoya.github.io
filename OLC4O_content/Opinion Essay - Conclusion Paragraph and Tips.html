Opinion Essay - Conclusion Paragraph and Tips

In this last part of the opinion essay lesson, we will learn about the concluding paragraph and some tips in writing opinion essays.

Conclusion

The conclusion is similar to the introduction, but its purpose is to summarize your reader’s argument. This is your last time to prove your point—make it count! You should restate your argument, as well as your points of proof, in different words.

Concluding sentence

Restate your thesis/opinion using different words.


You want to remind the reader about what they read.

Summarize the second to third reasons for your opinion.

Leave them with your opinion and leave them convinced that your opinion is correct.


End your final paragraph on a strong note, with a clincher (a final sentence that you use to get your point across).

Example:

Teenagers must have cell-phones. There are many reasons why all teenagers should have a cell phone. One reason is that it helps teenagers to stay safe. A second reason would be that it allows teens to keep in contact with those closest to them. A final reason is that cell-phones have many useful education applications available on them. Truly, cell-phones will help teenagers remain safe, be more responsible, and stay in touch with the people they care about.

Restate thesis (your opinion):

Again, I believe that parents should be held responsible for their children’s crimes.

Restate Blueprint (sum up your KEYPOINTS):

To sum up, parents should be held accountable because they ultimately make the household rules, it will encourage parents to make rules and children are too young to receive proper consequences in the court of law.

Concluding sentence:

Moreover, I strongly believe that parents should be held responsible for their children’s crimes.

Include a connection to your community or country or even the world to persuade your adult audience:

In Ottawa, there is an increase in teen violence, and the community needs to take steps to fix this issue.

 

Remember

The conclusion is very similar to the introduction, except it summarizes/restates your opinion and reasons. The decision DOES NOT have a hook.

Check your work for opportunities to do revisions. Here’s a sample checklist to use in revising or editing your work.

Series of Paragraphs Checklist

  • I have written at least 5 paragraphs, including an introduction, three body paragraphs and a conclusion.
  • My opinion is stated clearly in the opening paragraph. 
  • I have included at least 1-2 points that support my perspective in my body paragraphs.
  • I have explained each of my reasons, and they support my perspective.
  • My concluding paragraph summarizes my perspective and my reasons.
  • My spelling, grammar and sentence usage are proper and used effectively.
  • My tone is appropriate for my audience.

The use of I in writing

  • Be mindful of the use of I in your writing because it can make your argument sound overly biased.
  • The insertion of I into a sentence alters the way a sentence might sound and the composition of the sentence itself. I is often the subject of a sentence. If the essay’s subject is supposed to be, say, smoking, then by inserting yourself into the sentence, you are effectively displacing the subject of the essay into a secondary position. 

In the following example, the subject of the sentence is bolded and underlined:


Smoking is bad. vs.  I think smoking is bad.

In the first sentence, the rightful subject, smoking, is in the subject position the sentence. In the second sentence, I and I’s insertion replace tobacco as the subject, which draws attention to me and away from the topic that is supposed to be discussed. 

Remember to keep the message (the subject) and the messenger (the writer) separate.

Tip: “Prove” -ing your point

The word “prove” is frequently used in the discussion of persuasive writing. Writers may claim that one piece of evidence or another proves the argument, but proving an argument is often not possible. No evidence proves a debatable topic one way or the other; that is why the topic is debatable. Facts can be confirmed, but opinions can only be supported, explained, and persuaded.
