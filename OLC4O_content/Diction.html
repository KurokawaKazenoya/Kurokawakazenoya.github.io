Diction

Searching for words is not only something that you do in puzzles. When you speak or write in real life, you also carefully pick the words you would use. Knowing which words to use is proper diction.

Why does diction matter?

Without proper diction, your message can be misinterpreted by your audience.

Types of diction

The situation influences diction. For instance, you speak differently with friends compared to the way you would talk to your teachers.

There are three types of diction: 

  • Formal 
  • Informal 
  • Colloquial

Formal diction

Formal diction is used for formal settings such as meetings, lectures, press conferences and business correspondence. 

Informal diction

Informal diction is more appropriate for informal conversations with peers or friends. 

Colloquial Diction

Colloquial diction describes words that are used in a localized, highly informal gathering or social environment. It includes slang terms.

Denotation vs. Connotation

Denotation is the strict meaning of a word from the dictionary, while connotation is the emotional and imaginative association surrounding a word. 

 
For example, blue is a colour, but it is also used to describe feelings of sadness. 

The power of connotation

Connotations can be either positive, negative or neutral.

An understanding of words and their connotative meaning can improve a speaker’s diction. It can also give a persuasive edge to the message being delivered.

Clarity of Speech

Earlier, you learned that diction also refers to how clearly words are pronounced. Creating distinct sounds when speaking, is called articulation.

Mispronounced words weaken a speaker’s credibility. When words are not correctly articulated, it may distract the listener from the message.
