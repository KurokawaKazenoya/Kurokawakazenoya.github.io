Writing

  

All writers (especially the good ones) use a process when they write.

Because our minds don’t always think in logical paragraphs with supporting details and a carefully planned-out thesis statement, we have to approach writing one step at a time. This is called The Writing Process.

  

There are five stages involved in the writing process:

Prewriting      Drafting     Revising      Proofreading     Final draft

1. Prewriting

Let’s talk about them in detail. The first step is PREWRITING.

This is everything done before writing the first draft. Begin by finding out what purpose your writing is for, so you will be more focused.

  • Ask yourself, “Am I writing to...
  • Educate others or teach something.
  • Tell a story.
  • Express an emotion or opinion, or
  • Entertain others?”

What should I write first?

  • Most prolific writers would keep an idea list where they put anything that inspires them. You can make your own based on what you see, hear, or feel.

Here’s the fun part: it doesn’t need to be in full sentences yet.;

When prewriting, you can:

  • Make a list
  • Draw quick sketches
  • Create a mind map or a graphic organizer

Anything to keep the ideas going and later head to the next step.

A quick tip: Research is the key

Sometimes, you may not be that familiar with the topic yet. Research is key to getting details to support your thesis statement. Just make sure you cite your sources and references when appropriate.

2. Drafting

This is where you put together the ideas and write the first copy of your piece.

There’s no right or wrong way to draft. The goal is to match the type of piece you have chosen.

In the drafting stage, there is no need to get the order right immediately.

Some writers like to create from the beginning and proceed logically.

Others go the other way round, writing the ending first and then going backward.

Some write paragraphs on multiple sheets of colored paper and rearrange them later.

The draft would later be edited, so if you are writing on lined paper, you may want to skip some spaces where you can write changes or add notes.

If you are typing on a computer, you can make it double-spaced and print it out.

Are you making mistakes in the first draft? No problem!

There’s no need to fix anything just yet at this stage. Don’t worry about grammar, spelling, or punctuation.

Lose control and get those words on the page. The importance of the drafting stage is to get the ideas out and start writing.

3. Revising

This is where we make the draft better.

Read what you have written again.

Ask yourself:

  • Am I using the same sentence order structure time and time again?
  • Do all written parts support the purpose?
  • Are there unclear or overused words in the text?

Adjust the sentences or lines as you go. Once you’re done, read your writing aloud (if you are allowed to) to be sure it flows smoothly.

4. Proofreading

This is when you carefully check for any remaining errors, such as misspelled words, misplaced punctuation, and stylistic inconsistencies.

Watch out for:

  • Sentence fragments
  • Incorrect punctuation&nbsp;
  • Subject-verb agreement
  • Misspellings
  • Commonly confused words(e.g. Their, they’re, there)

Read your work out loud. If there is significant confusion in the text, you may have to go back to the previous step of revising the piece.
