Reading Strategies: Informational Texts

   

If you have to pick one of these two books, which one would you choose to read?

    

  

One book is the fictional story of a boy bitten by a radioactive spider, while the other contains illustrations of the natural history of spiders.

 

Spiders of the World: A Natural History is an example of an informational text.

It is not surprising that many people would rather follow the crime-fighting adventures of Miles Morales than sit down and read a book explaining where spiders came from.

Often, we think of informational text as hard to understand and even boring, but it can be fun too!

   

  

What to Expect in the Exam

In the OSSLT, you will be asked to read and understand two types of informational texts: an information paragraph or a news report.

  

Information paragraph News report

The information paragraph consists of 225-250 words. It gives you ideas and information about a specific topic which are based on facts.

You will also be asked to interpret sample news reports ranging from 225-250 words. You might see a news story in The Toronto Star, National Post, or The Globe and Mail.

   

How to Read Informational Texts

Here are some strategies that you could apply before, during, and after reading informational texts.

Before reading

Let’s take a look at what you can do before you start reading any type of informational text.

Tip #1

  • Set a purpose for reading. Ask yourself why you are reading this particular text.

Select each item to learn more.

Common structures

The primary purpose of the informational text is to inform. To achieve this, it follows one or more of five structures.

Description

Includes details to help you picture or get to know a person, place, thing, or idea. 

Sequence of events

Describes events in the order in which they happen.

Cause & effect

Explain why something happened (cause) and what happened as a result (effect).

Compare & contrast

Presents the similarities and/or differences between two items: a pair of events, period, ideas, people, places, etc.

 

How to Read Informational Texts

Knowing the structure will give you a better understanding of what you are about to read.

  

Accordion

Select each item to learn more.

Tip #2

Look over the text to see which elements appear.

  • Are there headings, subheadings, illustrations, or captions?

Tip #3

Examine the titles, headings and subheadings and scan for words that stand out.

Tip #4

Look for words and phrases that might give you clues about how the information is organized.

Tip #5

Read any overviews, summaries or questions. In a shorter piece, read the opening and concluding sentences or paragraphs.

Tip #6

Examine each illustration and read the titles and captions.

Tip #7

Recall what you already know about the topic.

Tip #8

Record some questions you might have about the topic.

Pre-reading activity

Answer the following questions about the informational text that you just skimmed.
  • What do you think is the author’s purpose in writing this article?
  • What words and phrases stood out for you?
  • What questions might you have based on your initial idea of the article?

   

During Reading

After your pre-reading activities, here are some strategies that you can apply while reading the informational text.

  

Tip #1 Tip #2 Tip #3

Divide the reading task into smaller chunks. Read a fragment, pause and think about what you read, and write a brief one-sentence summary or short point-form notes to help you remember essential and exciting information.

Read quickly, then slowly. Skim the sections you think will support your purpose for reading. When you find the specific information you want, slow down and read it word by word. You may need to reread the passage several times.

Read the selection and jot down thoughts, responses to your questions, and new questions that occur to you. 

  

Share your notes

Read the informational text again and record your notes here as you read. Start applying the strategies that you just learned.

  

After Reading

Now that you’re done reading, how can you make the best sense of what you just read? Try these two strategies.

Tip #1

  • Make connections to what you already know about the topic. How does the information you have read add to or alter what you learned about the topic?

Tip #2

  • Refer to the text to find evidence to support your response to the text.

  

Post-reading activity

Reflect on what you just read. How does the information you have read add or alter what you know about the topic?
