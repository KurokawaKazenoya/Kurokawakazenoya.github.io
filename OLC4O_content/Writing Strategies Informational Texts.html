Writing Strategies: Informational Texts

      

Now that you know the writing process and how to summarize the text. It’s time for you to work on different exercises for writing. We will start with Information Writing.

     

What is Information Writing?

Information writing gives the reader facts about a place, a person, an animal, etc.

Where do we find the informative text?

  • Encyclopedias, atlases, almanacs, dictionaries, thesauruses, magazines, textbooks, cookbooks, written directions, and websites all contain informative text.

    

   

Why is information writing important?

Information writing aims to:

  • provide facts in a way that is educational and purposeful
  • expose the truth through a reliable source
  • explain a fact
  • inform a reader about a specific topic

   

There are multiple ways to approach information writing:

Description

The author describes the topic.

Sequence

The author uses a numerical or chronological order to list items or events.

Compare/contrast

The author compares and contrasts two or more similar events, topics, or objects.

Cause/effect

The author delineates one or more causes and then describes the ensuing effects.

Problem/solution

The author poses a problem or question and then answers.

Research.         Research.            Research.

The reader may or may not have prior knowledge about the topic being discussed, so research is central to successfully drafting your work. The objective is to explain and analyze information by presenting an idea, relevant evidence, and appropriate discussion.

    

Writing the information paragraph

After compiling information, sort the information gathered into categories Each paragraph follows the formula:

Topic sentence > Supporting detail > Conclusion

Plan the paragraph. You may use a graphic organizer as a guide. Write your information paragraph about the topic and category you chose. Use your paragraph planner to guide you and include transition words.
