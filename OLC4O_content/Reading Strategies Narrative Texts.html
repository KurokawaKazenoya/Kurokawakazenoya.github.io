What to expect in the exam?

In the OSSLT, you will be asked to read and understand two types of narrative texts: dialogue and life narrative.

  

Dialogue Real-Life Narrative

A dialogue is a conversation between two people, sometimes more. Dialogues in the OSSLT are typically made up of 225-250 words.

Real-life narratives are longer, with a 550-600 word count. It is a story or an account of a significant event or an important moment in someone’s life.

Reading Narrative Texts

Aside from PACTS, there are other strategies that you can use when reading narrative texts.

Tip #1

  • Read the title and think about what might happen in the story.

What is the text about?

  • Does the title suggest any connections to your own life or raise any questions?

Before reading

Let’s take a look at what you can do before you start reading narrative texts.

Tip #2

  • Look at any illustrations.
  • What do they tell you about the story or subject?

Tip #3

  • Look the text over and sample the text to note its length, organization, level of language, and structure.
  • Pay attention to punctuation.

During Reading

After your pre-reading activities, here are some strategies that you can apply when you start reading narrative texts.

Tip #1

  • As you read, ask questions about what is happening. 
  • Make predictions about what might happen next. 

Tip #2

  • Form opinions about what is going on. Think about your responses and reactions to what you are reading.
  • Making notes in the margins and marking the text with your answers can help you focus your thinking.

Tip #3

  • Picture the setting, events, or images in your mind. As you read, imagine how the words will be spoken and see the action.

Tip #4

  • Make connections to what you already know.

Tip #5

  • Who are the people, and how are they related to each other?
  • Put yourself in their place. What would you say or do?

After Reading

Now that you’re done reading, how can you make the best sense of what you just read? Try these two strategies.

Tip #1

  • Use a graphic organizer to illustrate the story’s plot or sequence of events.

Tip #2

  • Retell or summarize the content in your own words, orally or in writing.

    

Post-Reading Activity

  • Create a graphic organizer illustrating the sequence of events in the narrative.
  • Write a summary of the narrative text using your own words below the graphic organizer you made.
