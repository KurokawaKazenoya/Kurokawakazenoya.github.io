Run-on Sentences

To further understand what run-on sentences are, we need to refresh our knowledge of clauses.

Clauses

  • The basic form of a clause is composed of a group of words that contain a subject and a verb.
  • For example.
  • He had  a very long life.
  • Note: Sentences have at least one main clause.

There are two basic types of clauses, independent clauses and dependent clauses.

Independent clauses are sentences that make sense on their own, with a subject and a verb.

  • She ate a ham sandwich.
  • They ran towards me

Dependent clauses are sentences that also have a subject and a verb but are not complete.

  • Because she is afraid of cats.
  • When I get there.

Run-on Sentences

  • Happen when two or more independent clauses are combined without proper punctuation marks or coordinating conjunctions.

For example,

I love reading books I spend all day reading novels!

Using a semicolon to separate two independent clauses is also grammatically correct. It shows a close relationship between the two independent clauses.

For example:

  • I love reading books; I spend all day reading novels!

Try correcting this sentence:

I’m often tired, I walk home from school every day.

The error in the sentence is called a “comma splice.”

  • A typical example of a run-on sentence is a comma splice.

A comma splice is an error that happens when two independent clauses are joined only by a comma.

For example,

  • It rained hard during the concert, everybody decided to leave early.

Adding a coordinating conjunction after the comma fixes this error.

  • It rained hard during the concert, so everybody decided to leave early.

Note that for this to work, one sentence should be the outcome or the effect of the other.

When using a transitional word, it is important to include a semicolon and a comma after the transitional expression.

Adding a semicolon fixes this sentence.

  • The proposal didn’t go according to plan, therefore the whole event was cancelled.

Adding a semicolon fixes this sentence.

  • The proposal didn’t go according to plan; therefore, the whole event was cancelled.

Sentence fragments are a combination of words that do not form one complete sentence. They are phrases and dependent clauses that may be missing a noun, verb or may not express a complete idea.

For example,

 

  • Because of the constant noise.

On its own, it doesn’t form a complete thought. For it to become a complete sentence, additional information is needed. E.g. Because of the constant noise, the police were called.

Here, the dependent clause answers the independent clause.

Fused Sentences

  • Are clauses that are joined directly without punctuation marks or conjunctions.
  • I thought I saw a pussy cat I thought you saw it too.

Misplaced Modifiers

  •  Are words, clauses, or phrases that are incorrectly used. These are words that are separated from the words that they describe.
  • There are several types of misplaced modifiers; these may be adjectives, adverbs, phrases, or clauses.

Misplaced adjectives are adjectives that are separated from the nouns they should be modifying. Most of the time, it changes the meaning of the sentence.

For example,

 

  • The kid was eating a Hot dish of pumpkin soup for dinner.

Correctly place the adjective before the noun it modifies.

 

  • The kid was eating a dish of Hot pumpkin soup for dinner.
Here, the pumpkin soup is the one that is “hot,” not the dish.

Misplaced Adverbs

also change the meaning of a sentence.

For example,

Only Alex was brought to the office.

  • This means that Alex was the only one brought to the office.
  • But if you change the placement of the word “only,” the meaning also changes.

Alex was brought only to the office.

  • This means that Alex was only brought to the office, nowhere else.
  • These sentences make sense, although the meaning of the word depends on where you place the adverb.

 Another example would be:

I ate the food that I brought to school quickly.

  • This means that you ate the food that you quickly brought to school.

Instead, say: I quickly ate the food that I brought to school.

  • The meaning now changes to YOU eating the food QUICKLY.

Does this sentence have a dangling modifier?

While studying for the OSSLT in his room, an idea passed through his mind.

Dangling Modifiers
The sentence is a dangling modifier because it does not describe WHO was studying for the OSSLT.

  • To correct this,

i. While studying for the OSSLT in his room, he had an idea pass through his mind.

ii. Here it describes WHO is studying for the test, which is he.
