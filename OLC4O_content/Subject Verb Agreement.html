Subject Verb Agreement

  

Phrases and clauses between the subject and the verb will not change the number of the subject.

For example, a phrase in the middle of the subject and a verb doesn’t change the number of the subject:

  • This box of white grapes fits perfectly in my cupboard.
  • Here, the subject “box” is the one that “fits” perfectly in the cupboard.

The case is the same for clauses:

  • The people who attended the seminar were amused.

      

Singular and plural indefinite pronouns take singular and plural verbs, respectively.

If your subject is a singular indefinite pronoun, it should have a singular verb.

  • (e.g. each, either, neither, one, no one, nobody, nothing, anyone, anybody, anything, someone, somebody, something, everyone, everybody, everything)

  

Singular indefinite pronoun: singular verb

Pronoun: each, either, neither, one, no one, nobody, nothing, anyone, anybody, anything, someone, somebody, something, everyone, everybody, everything

Example:

  • Everyone does their own thing at work.

  

Singular and plural indefinite pronouns take singular and plural verbs, respectively.

If your subject is a plural indefinite pronoun, then it should use a plural verb.

  • (e.g. several, few, many, both)

   

Plural indefinite pronoun: plural verb

Pronoun: several, few, many, both

Example:

  • Many do their job properly at work.

  

Singular and plural indefinite pronouns take singular and plural verbs, respectively.

Some indefinite pronouns can either be singular or plural. This depends on the countability of the subject (e.g. some, any, none, all, most).

If it’s uncountable, you use a singular verb.

  • Most of the salt is on the counter.
  • Since salt is uncountable, a singular verb was used.

If it’s countable, you use a plural verb.

  • Some of the pens are on the table.
  • Since the pens are countable, a plural verb was used.

Compound subjects joined by and are always plural.

For example,

  • The teacher and the students are discussing the lesson.

 

Plural indefinite pronoun: plural verb

Compound subjects joined by or/nor use verbs depending on the subject closer to the verb.

  • Neither the teacher nor the students are aware of the mistake.
  • Since the subject “students” is closer to the verb, a plural verb was used.

      

A sentence with an inverted structure must also agree with the verb.

For example,

  • There is a person in the field.
  • There are people in the field.

   

The titles of one single unit are always singular.

For example:

  • American Gods is one of the best Neil Gaiman books.

Subjects with a plural form can either take a singular or plural verb depending on the subject’s meaning.

  • If the subject has a singular meaning, you use a singular verb.

For example,

  • Statistics is my favorite topic.

Here, the word “statistics” is singular; therefore, a singular verb is used.

Subjects with a plural form can either take a singular or plural verb depending on the subject’s meaning.

If the subject has a plural meaning, then you use a plural verb.

  • Statistics show that people love using their smartphones.

Here the word “statistics” is plural; therefore, a plural verb is used.

        

Special cases:

Using The followed by “number of” uses a singular verb.

For example,

  • The number of kids in school increases.

Using A followed by “number of” uses plural verbs.

For example,

  • A number of students cut class
