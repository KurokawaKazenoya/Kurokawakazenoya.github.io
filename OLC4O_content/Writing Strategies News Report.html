Writing Strategies: News Report

From stalls everywhere to the television and online, you might have seen this form of writing. This is called the news report. In this lesson, we will focus on news reports and how to write them.

What is a news report?

A news report is an Objective (fact-, not opinion-based) piece of writing."

It includes several paragraphs, but it is intended to be short (generally a page long).

The purpose of a newspaper article is to provide an account of the events that have occurred. It delivers facts to the reader about something that is happening (or happened) in our society. It answers the questions of who, what, when, where, why, and how (5Ws).

Why write a newspaper article?

Writing a newspaper article helps you practice summarizing the main ideas from an event and practice re-telling an event WITHOUT adding your personal bias (or perspective).

Callout

Steps to Writing a Newspaper Article

  • All News Reports should begin with a headline, a title, which gives the reader a short introduction to the topic. The headline should be unique and catchy.
  • News reports also have a byline that indicates the name of the writer.
  • Encircle the headline and put a blue underline to identify the byline.

The first paragraph should be a general overview of the topic.

It should contain a general reference to all of the Ws (which you will expand upon later).

Example:
Students at St Francis Xavier are preparing for their second annual annual Coyote Prowl, taking place on Friday, October 14, 2011. This event was created to raise funds for the school and students.

The next few paragraphs should expand upon each of the Ws in more detail. These paragraphs will be short and only provide the reader with the necessary information.

Write down the answer to the five Ws (and H). Focus on one “W” in each paragrap.

Who  What  Where  When  Why  How

Example body paragraphs

Accordion

Select each item to learn more.

Who

The Coyote Prowl is run by the students at St. Francis Xavier, including members of the Student and Athletic Councils. “It’s great that it’s run by students, to provide money for students” says John Smith, a member of this year’s athletic council. “It’s not only a fundraiser but a really fun day for all students to meet each other and see the neighbourhood” says Andrea Johnson, a student at St. Francis Xavier.

What

This fundraiser is the brainchild of Principal Michael Curry. It was created last year to help raise funds to support areas of the school. It was called the Coyote Prowl as the school teams are called the Coyotes. The students are asked to raise funds, all of which are donated directly back to the school. The students then “Prowl” through the surrounding community.

When

This year’s Prowl will be taking place on October 14, 2011. “We like to run the Prowl at the beginning of the year as it is not only an excellent fundraiser, but also a great way to build community within our growing school.” says Curry. Students will be raising funds in the weeks leading up to this date.

Where

The Prowl will be taking place at St. Francis Xavier High School, and through the surrounding community of Riverside South. Many residents welcome the students, including Andrea Ryan, “The kids were very spirited and friendly as they came by my house last year, and I look forward to their return this year.”

Why

The Prowl is meant to raise money for many programs at the school. Last year one of the recipients was the drama program, and the extra money helped enhance the production of Dr. Faustus. Students are given the opportunity to provide input as to where the money is distributed through student surveys.

How

The funds are raised by the students accessing people they know in the community to provide them with funds. This includes family, friends, neighbours and anyone who wishes to donate. As well, in previous years a number of businesses in the community have provided support. “It really is a whole-community effort,” says Curry.

Reminders when writing a news article:

  • Make sure you answer all of your Ws (and H!)—you may want to brainstorm beforehand to ensure all are addressed.
  • Use quotes from participants and other facts to support your 5Ws.
  • Give details of the participant you quote (name, age, occupation etc.)
  • Make sure you are always writing in the third person (he, she, they). DO NOT USE "I"
  • Write a conclusion to sum up, your article. Make sure to conclude the main ideas from the event.
