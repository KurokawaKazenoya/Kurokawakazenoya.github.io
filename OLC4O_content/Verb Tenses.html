Verb Tenses

        

Verbs

We use the word "verb" to describe both acts (what things do) and states of being (how things are).

     

Verbs change depending on their tenses. There are three different verb tenses:

   

  

The present tense is used when you’re talking about the present, but it is also used to talk about the future.

    

There are four forms of the present tense; let’s use an example to show how these forms change:

  • Simple present: I study
  • Present perfect: I have studied
  • Present continuous: I am studying
  • Present perfect continuous: I have been studying

Now that you’ve reviewed the present tense. Let’s move on to the past tense.

          

The past tense is used to talk about the past and the future in theory.

There are four forms of the past tense; let’s use an example to show how these forms change.

  • Past simple - I studied.
  • Past perfect tense - I had studied.
  • Past continuous tense - I was studying.
  • Past perfect continuous tense - I had been studying.

Let’s move on to the future tenses and how they work.

        

The future tense is used for describing future activities or states.

Like the other verb tenses, there are also four forms. Let’s use an example to show how these forms change.

  • Simple future - I will study.
  • Future perfect - I will have studied.
  • Future continuous - I will be studying.
  • Future perfect continuous - I will have been studying.

      

Simple Future Tense Future Perfect Tense Future Continuous Tense Future Perfect Continuous Tense

-is the will and the base form of a verb.

The simple future tense is used to express something that hasn’t happened yet or something that will begin and end in the future.

For example:

  • I will study for the exam tonight.

However, there is another way to state this, which follows the formula of am/is/are followed by ending with the root form.

For example:

  • I am going to study tonight.

On the other hand, the simple future negative is simply the addition of “not” between will and the root verb, or between am/is/are and going to.

  • I will not study tonight for the exam.
  • I am not going to study for the exam.

The future perfect tense is a grammatical tense used to describe an action that will be completed in the future before another point. It is formed by combining the future tense of the auxiliary verb "will" or "shall" with the present perfect form of the main verb.

For example:

  • By next year, she will have visited five different countries.
